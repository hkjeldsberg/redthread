## 1. The Menstrual Cycle and Its Phases

For the foundational biology of the cycle, stick to peer-reviewed medical databases and public health organizations.

* **Mayo Clinic:** Provides a clear breakdown of the **Follicular**, **Ovulatory**, and **Luteal** phases.
* **Office on Women's Health (WomensHealth.gov):** Offers comprehensive guides on what constitutes a "normal" cycle and the hormonal triggers for menstruation.
* **The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG):** The gold standard for clinical definitions and hormonal health.

---

## 2. Exercise Intensity and Timing

Training "with your cycle" (often called cycle syncing) focuses on maximizing strength when estrogen is high and prioritizing recovery when progesterone rises.

* **The Female Athlete Podcast / Dr. Stacy Sims:** Dr. Sims is a prominent researcher in female physiology. Her mantra, *"Women are not small men,"* focuses on how the high-hormone (luteal) phase increases body temperature and changes sweat rates.
* **National Academy of Sports Medicine (NASM):** They provide evidence-based articles on how to adjust resistance training and cardio based on the cycle.
* **Key Concepts:**
* **Follicular Phase:** Focus on high-intensity interval training (HIIT) and heavy lifting (higher pain tolerance and better power output).
* **Luteal Phase:** Shift toward steady-state cardio, mobility work, and lower-intensity strength as the body's core temperature rises.



---

## 3. Nutrition and Cycle Phases

Nutrition needs shift as the body prepares for potential pregnancy or menstruation, particularly regarding insulin sensitivity and cravings.

* **Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (eatright.org):** Offers science-based advice on iron intake and managing PMS through diet.
* **Healthline (Medical Review Board):** Look for articles reviewed by Registered Dietitians (RDs) regarding cycle-mapping for micronutrients.
* **General Nutritional Focus:**
* **Follicular:** Focus on complex carbs to fuel higher-intensity workouts.
* **Luteal:** Increase healthy fats and protein. The body’s basal metabolic rate can increase by **5–10%** during this phase, often requiring a slight increase in caloric intake.



---

## 4. Fasting Windows and Hormonal Health

Fasting is the most sensitive topic regarding the menstrual cycle. Aggressive fasting can sometimes trigger the "stress" hormone cortisol, which may disrupt the GnRH pulses required for ovulation.

* **Dr. Mindy Pelz (The Reset Academy):** While more focused on lifestyle application, she provides specific protocols (like the "Fast Like a Girl" framework) that suggest avoiding long fasts during the week before your period.
* **The Jean Hailes Foundation:** A leading women’s health organization that discusses the impact of energy availability on hormonal regularity.
* **Suggested Guidelines:**
* **Days 1–12 (Follicular):** Generally considered the safest time for intermittent fasting (e.g., 13–16 hours) as the body is more resilient to stress.
* **Days 13–15 (Ovulation):** Caution is advised; high estrogen can make the body more sensitive to cortisol.
* **Day 21–Start of Period (Luteal):** Most experts recommend **reducing or stopping** fasts during this "progesterone-building" phase to prevent hormonal down-regulation.
